Lecture 1: Foundations of PFCC

Slide 1 of 5

Historical Development of PFCC

Patient and Family-Centered Care (PFCC) emerged from several converging influences:

  • Consumer rights movement of the 1960s-70s emphasized patient autonomy
  • Family advocacy, particularly in pediatrics, challenged restrictive visitation policies
  • Research demonstrating improved outcomes with family involvement
  • Growing recognition of the ethical imperative to involve patients in their care

The Institute for Patient- and Family-Centered Care (IPFCC) was established in 1992 to advance the understanding and practice of patient and family-centered care.

Definition and Core Concepts

PFCC is an approach to the planning, delivery, and evaluation of healthcare that is grounded in mutually beneficial partnerships among healthcare providers, patients, and families.

The Four Core Principles:
  1. Dignity and Respect: Honoring patient and family perspectives and choices
  2. Information Sharing: Communicating complete and unbiased information with patients and families
  3. Participation: Encouraging and supporting patient and family participation in care and decision-making
  4. Collaboration: Involving patients and families in program and policy development, implementation, and evaluation

Evidence Base for PFCC

Research has demonstrated numerous benefits associated with PFCC approaches:

Category Benefits
Patient Outcomes Improved clinical outcomes, better symptom management, reduced anxiety
Experience of Care Higher satisfaction, increased trust in healthcare providers, better adherence to treatment plans
Provider Experience Improved job satisfaction, reduced burnout, enhanced communication skills
Healthcare System Reduced readmissions, decreased length of stay, more efficient resource utilization

Note: The strength of evidence varies across different settings and interventions.

Defining "Family" in PFCC

"Family is defined by the patient. Family members are individuals who are related in any way—biologically, legally, or emotionally. Patients define their 'family' and determine how they will participate in care and decision-making."

— Institute for Patient- and Family-Centered Care

This inclusive definition:

  • Respects diverse family structures and relationships
  • Acknowledges the patient as the expert in identifying their support network
  • Avoids making assumptions based on legal or biological relationships
  • Recognizes that family involvement should be determined by the patient

The level of family involvement may vary based on patient preferences, cultural factors, and the nature of healthcare decisions.

PFCC Across the Continuum of Care

PFCC principles can be applied across all healthcare settings and throughout the care journey:

Primary Care
  • Shared decision-making
  • Family-inclusive health education
  • Accessible communication channels
Acute Care
  • Family presence policies
  • Bedside rounds with family
  • Care transitions planning
Long-term Care
  • Resident and family councils
  • Personalized care planning
  • Maintaining family connections

Successful implementation requires organizational commitment, leadership support, and ongoing education for healthcare providers.

Lecture 2: Core Principles in Practice

Slide 1 of 5

Dignity and Respect in Practice

The principle of dignity and respect requires healthcare providers to:

  • Listen to and honor patient and family perspectives and choices
  • Incorporate patient and family knowledge, values, beliefs, and cultural backgrounds into care planning
  • Recognize patients and families as individuals with unique needs and preferences
  • Avoid making assumptions about capabilities, values, or desires
Practical Examples:
  • Addressing patients by their preferred name and title
  • Providing appropriate privacy during examinations and procedures
  • Respecting cultural practices regarding modesty, gender of providers, or family involvement
  • Asking patients how they wish to be included in decisions about their care

Information Sharing in Practice

Effective information sharing means communicating and sharing complete and unbiased information with patients and families in ways that are affirming and useful.

Key Components:
  • Providing timely, complete, and accurate information
  • Using plain language (not medical jargon)
  • Confirming understanding (teach-back method)
  • Offering information in preferred formats and languages
Common Barriers:
  • Time constraints during clinical encounters
  • Limited health literacy among patients
  • Language differences
  • Complex medical information

Effective information sharing reduces anxiety, improves adherence to treatment plans, and leads to better health outcomes.

Information sharing is bi-directional—providers must also be willing to receive and value information from patients and families.

Participation in Practice

The participation principle focuses on encouraging and supporting patients and families to participate in care and decision-making at the level they choose.

Spectrum of Participation:
Level Patient/Family Role Provider Support
Information Recipient Receives information about diagnosis, treatment options Provides clear, comprehensive information
Consultant Provides input about preferences, experiences Actively solicits and considers patient/family input
Co-designer Collaborates in developing care plan Facilitates meaningful involvement in decision-making
Partner in Care Actively participates in implementing care Teaches skills, provides resources and support

Patients and families should be supported to participate at whatever level feels right for them, which may change over time or with different aspects of care.

Collaboration in Practice

Collaboration extends beyond individual care to include patients and families working with healthcare providers on:

  • Program and policy development
  • Facility design
  • Professional education
  • Care delivery
  • Quality improvement
Examples of Collaborative Structures:
  1. Patient and Family Advisory Councils (PFACs): Formal groups that meet regularly to provide input on policies, programs, and practices
  2. Patient Experience Committees: Focus on improving service quality and patient experience
  3. Quality and Safety Teams: Include patient/family representatives in efforts to enhance care quality and safety
  4. Design Teams: Involve patients/families in facility design or redesign projects

Effective collaboration requires genuine partnership, where patients and families are valued for their unique perspectives and contributions.

Implementing PFCC Principles: Practical Strategies

Successfully implementing PFCC principles requires organizational commitment and practical strategies:

Clinical Level Strategies
  • Use shared decision-making tools
  • Conduct bedside rounds with patient/family
  • Implement open medical records
  • Create welcoming physical environments
  • Train staff in communication techniques
Organizational Level Strategies
  • Establish Patient/Family Advisory Councils
  • Include patients in quality improvement
  • Develop policies that support PFCC
  • Measure and report on PFCC metrics
  • Recognize and reward PFCC practices

Remember: PFCC is not a set of isolated tactics but a fundamental approach to care that requires integrating all four core principles—dignity and respect, information sharing, participation, and collaboration—into every aspect of healthcare.

Lecture 3: Communication Skills for PFCC

Slide 1 of 5

Core Communication Skills for PFCC

Effective communication is the foundation of patient and family-centered care. Key skills include:

Active Listening
  • Giving full attention to the speaker
  • Avoiding interruptions
  • Using body language that shows engagement
  • Focusing on understanding rather than responding
Empathic Response
  • Acknowledging emotions
  • Validating concerns
  • Demonstrating understanding
  • Responding with compassion
Clear Information Exchange
  • Using plain, non-technical language
  • Organizing information logically
  • Checking for understanding
  • Providing written resources
Shared Decision-Making
  • Presenting options clearly
  • Discussing benefits and risks
  • Exploring patient values and preferences
  • Supporting informed choices

Communication Tools and Techniques

Several evidence-based tools can enhance provider-patient-family communication:

The BATHE Technique

A framework for addressing psychosocial aspects of care:

  • Background: "What's going on in your life?"
  • Affect: "How do you feel about that?"
  • Trouble: "What troubles you most about this situation?"
  • Handling: "How are you handling that?"
  • Empathy: "That must be very difficult for you."
The Teach-Back Method

Confirming patient understanding:

  1. Explain information clearly
  2. Ask patients to explain in their own words
  3. Identify and address misunderstandings
  4. Repeat until shared understanding is achieved

"To make sure I explained things clearly, could you please tell me in your own words what we discussed about your treatment plan?"

Communicating in Challenging Situations

PFCC communication skills are especially important in difficult scenarios:

Delivering Bad News

The SPIKES Protocol:

  • Setting up the interview
  • Perception assessment
  • Invitation to share information
  • Knowledge transmission
  • Exploring emotions with empathy
  • Strategy and summary
Managing Conflict
  • Focus on interests, not positions
  • Separate people from problems
  • Explore options for mutual gain
  • Use objective criteria
  • Acknowledge emotions
  • Create a safe space for dialogue
Discussing Treatment Options
  • Present all reasonable options
  • Describe benefits and harms
  • Provide balanced information
  • Use decision aids when available
  • Allow time for deliberation
End-of-Life Conversations
  • Start early and revisit regularly
  • Explore goals of care
  • Discuss quality of life preferences
  • Be direct but compassionate
  • Involve appropriate family members

Addressing Communication Barriers

Several factors can impede effective communication in healthcare:

Barrier Strategies to Address
Language Differences
  • Use professional interpreter services
  • Provide translated written materials
  • Use visual aids and teach-back
Health Literacy Challenges
  • Use plain language (not medical jargon)
  • Focus on key points (3-5 main messages)
  • Employ visual aids and demonstration
Emotional Distress
  • Acknowledge emotions before sharing information
  • Break information into smaller segments
  • Offer to revisit discussion when ready
Cultural Differences
  • Learn about relevant cultural norms
  • Ask about preferences for communication
  • Involve cultural brokers when appropriate

Remember: Addressing barriers requires both system-level supports (e.g., interpreter services, accessible materials) and individual provider skills and awareness.

Building Communication Skills

Developing effective PFCC communication skills requires intentional practice and reflection:

Learning Approaches
  • Formal communication training
  • Simulation exercises
  • Role-playing difficult conversations
  • Observation and feedback
  • Video review of interactions
Reflective Practice
  • Self-assessment after interactions
  • Journaling about communication challenges
  • Peer coaching and feedback
  • Analysis of "difficult" conversations
  • Mindfulness practice

Key Reflection Questions:

  1. What went well in this interaction?
  2. What could I have done differently?
  3. How did I respond to emotional cues?
  4. Did I verify understanding effectively?
  5. How did I engage family members?

Communication skills develop over time with deliberate practice and reflection. Even experienced clinicians continue to refine these skills throughout their careers.

Creating PFCC-Oriented Clinical Processes

Clinical processes and workflows must be designed to incorporate PFCC principles:

Key Clinical Process Redesign Elements:
Process Traditional Approach PFCC Approach
Admissions Focus on data collection and administrative tasks Incorporation of patient preferences and needs assessment
Rounds Clinician-only discussions outside patient rooms Bedside rounds with patient and family participation
Care Planning Clinician-directed with minimal patient input Collaborative process with shared decision-making
Handoffs Between providers only Include patient and family in transitions of care
Discharge Instructions provided at time of departure Process begins at admission with ongoing education

Successful process redesign requires input from patients, families, frontline staff, and leadership to ensure changes are both patient-centered and operationally feasible.

Patient and Family Advisory Councils (PFACs)

PFACs are structured bodies that bring patient and family perspectives into organizational planning and decision-making:

PFAC Structure
  • Diverse membership reflecting community
  • Clear roles and responsibilities
  • Regular meeting schedule
  • Staff liaison and leadership involvement
  • Formal reporting structure
PFAC Functions
  • Review and provide input on policies
  • Participate in facility design planning
  • Contribute to quality improvement
  • Assist with educational initiatives
  • Identify service gaps and solutions
PFAC Implementation Success Factors:
  1. Clear charter and purpose
  2. Thoughtful recruitment and orientation
  3. Meaningful projects with visible outcomes
  4. Administrative support and resources
  5. Leadership engagement and responsiveness
  6. Regular evaluation of impact and effectiveness

PFACs represent one of the most concrete demonstrations of organizational commitment to the collaboration principle of PFCC.

Technology and Information Systems for PFCC

Information technology can either support or hinder PFCC implementation:

Patient Portals and Digital Access
  • Secure messaging with providers
  • Access to test results and records
  • Appointment scheduling
  • Proxy access for family caregivers
  • Medication management tools
Bedside Technology
  • Digital whiteboards with care team info
  • Patient education systems
  • Remote family participation in rounds
  • Goal tracking and progress displays
  • Patient-reported outcomes collection
PFCC-Oriented EHR Design Principles:
  1. Capture patient goals, values, and preferences
  2. Document family involvement and contact information
  3. Support shared care planning functionality
  4. Generate patient-friendly educational materials
  5. Enable patient access to appropriate information
  6. Facilitate secure communication among care team, patient, and family

Technology should facilitate rather than replace meaningful human interactions, serving as a tool to enhance communication and collaboration.

Lecture 5: Measuring PFCC Outcomes

Slide 1 of 5

Framework for Measuring PFCC

A comprehensive measurement approach for PFCC includes multiple dimensions:

Structure Measures

Focus on system capabilities and resources

  • PFCC policies and procedures
  • Staff training in PFCC principles
  • Physical environment features
  • Information systems for patient access
Process Measures

Focus on implementation of PFCC practices

  • Family presence during rounds
  • Use of shared decision-making tools
  • Documentation of patient preferences
  • Family participation in care planning
Outcome Measures

Focus on results and impact

  • Patient/family experience ratings
  • Clinical outcomes (when appropriate)
  • Patient activation and engagement
  • Staff satisfaction and retention

Best Practice: Use a balanced set of metrics that span structure, process, and outcome domains to create a comprehensive picture of PFCC implementation and impact.

The most effective measurement approaches connect specific PFCC interventions to their intended outcomes through a clear theory of change.

Measuring Patient and Family Experience

Patient and family experience is a core outcome of PFCC implementation:

Common Experience Measurement Approaches
Method Strengths Limitations
Standardized Surveys
  • Consistent data collection
  • Benchmark comparisons
  • Validated questions
  • Limited depth
  • Response biases
  • Lag time in reporting
Real-time Feedback
  • Immediate actionability
  • High response rates
  • Point-of-care improvements
  • Limited standardization
  • Resource-intensive
  • Less comprehensive
Patient/Family Narratives
  • Rich contextual details
  • Identifies improvement opportunities
  • Powerful for staff engagement
  • Not easily quantifiable
  • Time-intensive analysis
  • Small sample sizes

Best practice is to use a combination of approaches to capture both breadth (through surveys) and depth (through narratives) of patient and family experiences.

Key Domains to Measure in Patient Experience:
  • Respect for values, preferences, and needs
  • Coordination and integration of care
  • Information and education
  • Physical comfort and emotional support
  • Involvement of family and friends
  • Continuity and transition

PFCC-Related Clinical and Operational Outcomes

PFCC implementation can impact a range of clinical and operational outcomes:

Evidence-Based PFCC Impact Areas
Clinical Outcomes
  • Improved medication adherence
  • Better symptom management
  • Reduced complications
  • Decreased readmissions
  • Lower infection rates (in some settings)
Operational Outcomes
  • Shorter lengths of stay
  • More efficient resource utilization
  • Reduced complaints and grievances
  • Lower staff turnover
  • Fewer malpractice claims
Measurement Considerations:
  1. Select outcomes that are logically connected to specific PFCC interventions
  2. Account for confounding factors that may influence outcomes
  3. Balance short and long-term outcome measures
  4. Consider both direct outcomes (experience) and indirect ones (clinical measures)
  5. Establish realistic timeframes for expected changes

While PFCC should be implemented primarily because it's the right approach to care, demonstrating its impact on clinical and operational outcomes can strengthen organizational commitment.

Measuring PFCC at Different Levels

PFCC measurement should occur at multiple levels of the healthcare system:

Individual Provider Level

Focus: Provider-patient/family interactions

  • Communication effectiveness
  • Shared decision-making practices
  • Patient feedback on individual providers
  • Self-assessment of PFCC skills

Methods: Observation, patient ratings, self-assessment, peer feedback

Unit/Department Level

Focus: Team-based PFCC implementation

  • Family presence policies
  • Bedside rounds participation
  • Environmental factors
  • Consistent PFCC practices

Methods: Audits, patient experience surveys, staff assessments, process measures

Organizational Level

Focus: Systemic PFCC implementation

  • Leadership commitment
  • Policy alignment with PFCC
  • Resource allocation
  • Training and support systems

Methods: Organizational assessment tools, policy review, culture surveys

PFCC Measurement Tools for Different Levels:
  • Individual: Patient-Centered Communication Tools (PCCT), CAT (Communication Assessment Tool)
  • Unit: Unit Practice Councils, Patient and Family Shadowing, Experience-Based Co-Design
  • Organization: Patient-Centered Medical Home Assessment (PCMH-A), PFCC Go Guide, Organizational Self-Assessment

Using Measurement Data for Improvement

Collecting data is only valuable if it drives meaningful improvement:

Data-to-Action Cycle
Step 1: Analyze Data
  • Review multiple data sources
  • Identify trends and patterns
  • Compare to benchmarks
  • Triangulate quantitative and qualitative data
Step 2: Identify Opportunities
  • Prioritize areas for improvement
  • Consider impact and feasibility
  • Involve patients/families in selection
  • Focus on meaningful changes
Step 3: Design Interventions
  • Co-design with patients/families
  • Consider evidence-based practices
  • Develop implementation plan
  • Establish measures of success
Step 4: Implement and Evaluate
  • Pilot test changes
  • Measure impact
  • Refine interventions
  • Scale successful practices
Best Practices for Using PFCC Data:
  1. Make data transparent and accessible to all stakeholders
  2. Present information in clear, actionable formats
  3. Involve patients and families in interpreting data
  4. Celebrate successes and improvements
  5. Use storytelling to bring data to life
  6. Create accountability for responding to findings

Effective use of measurement data creates a continuous learning and improvement cycle that advances PFCC implementation over time.

Remember that measurement should support - not replace - the fundamental human connections at the heart of patient and family-centered care.

Case Study 1: Family-Centered Care in a Pediatric ICU

Case Overview

Eight-year-old Sophia was admitted to the pediatric intensive care unit (PICU) following complications from an asthma attack. This case study examines how PFCC principles were applied during her two-week PICU stay.

Key Challenges

  • Balancing family presence with intensive medical interventions
  • Managing information sharing during a rapidly changing clinical situation
  • Supporting parents in decision-making during periods of uncertainty
  • Including siblings in the care process appropriately

PFCC Approaches Implemented

1. Unrestricted Family Presence

Parents were welcomed 24/7, with accommodations for one parent to sleep in the room. Siblings were allowed scheduled visits with preparation and support.

2. Family-Centered Rounds

Parents participated in daily medical rounds, encouraged to ask questions and share observations about their daughter's condition.

3. Family Decision Support

Decision aids and family meetings were used to help parents understand treatment options and participate in care planning.

4. Parent Participation in Care

Parents were taught to assist with aspects of Sophia's care, including comfort measures and recognition of distress signals.

Outcomes

Implementation of PFCC principles yielded several positive outcomes:

  • Increased parental confidence and reduced anxiety
  • Enhanced communication between the healthcare team and family
  • More efficient response to changes in Sophia's condition due to parental recognition of subtle signs
  • Smoother transition to home care following discharge
  • High parental satisfaction with care experience despite the stressful situation

Lessons Learned

  1. Involving parents as active team members improves quality of care and family experience
  2. Regular family meetings provide opportunities to address questions and concerns in a less hurried environment than daily rounds
  3. Supporting siblings is an often-overlooked aspect of family-centered care in critical care settings
  4. Staff education and support are essential for successful implementation of PFCC in high-acuity environments

Reflection Questions

  1. How might PFCC approaches need to be adapted in emergency situations?
  2. What systems and resources are needed to support meaningful family presence in critical care settings?
  3. How can healthcare providers balance family participation with their own clinical responsibilities?

Case Study 2: PFCC in a Long-Term Care Facility for Older Adults

Case Overview

Oakwood Terrace is a 120-bed long-term care facility that undertook a comprehensive initiative to transform its culture from an institutional model to one based on PFCC principles. This case study examines their three-year journey.

Initial Challenges

  • Institutional routines that prioritized staff efficiency over resident preferences
  • Limited family involvement in care planning and decision-making
  • Physical environment designed for surveillance rather than privacy and autonomy
  • High staff turnover and workforce shortages
  • Resistance to change among long-tenured staff

PFCC Approaches Implemented

1. Person-Directed Care Planning

Care plans were redesigned to focus on resident goals and preferences, including preferred daily routines, meaningful activities, and individual interests. Families were invited to participate in regular care planning meetings.

2. Resident and Family Council

A joint council with decision-making authority on facility policies was established, meeting monthly with leadership to address concerns and suggest improvements.

3. Consistent Assignment

Staff were assigned to the same residents consistently to foster relationships and better understanding of individual needs and preferences. This included restructuring schedules to support continuity.

4. Environmental Modifications

Physical spaces were redesigned to create small, home-like neighborhoods with private and communal areas. Residents were encouraged to personalize their living spaces.

Implementation Process

The facility followed a phased approach to transformation:

  1. Assessment (3 months): Conducted surveys and focus groups with residents, families, and staff to identify priorities. Evaluated existing policies against PFCC principles.
  2. Planning (6 months):
  3. Planning (6 months): Developed a three-year strategic plan with clear milestones. Created interdisciplinary teams for each major initiative.
  4. Implementation (2 years): Rolled out changes in manageable phases, starting with staff education and consistent assignment, then moving to care planning procedures and environmental modifications.
  5. Evaluation (ongoing): Established regular measurement of outcomes and continuous feedback mechanisms to assess progress and make adjustments.

Outcomes

After three years, the facility demonstrated several positive outcomes:

  • 25% increase in resident and family satisfaction scores
  • 30% reduction in staff turnover
  • 20% decrease in use of antipsychotic medications
  • 15% reduction in falls
  • 40% increase in resident participation in activities
  • Greater alignment between resident stated preferences and actual care provided

Challenges and Lessons Learned

Challenges
  • Initial staff resistance and fear of change
  • Regulatory requirements that seemed at odds with PFCC
  • Resource constraints for environmental modifications
  • Balancing individual preferences with group living realities
Lessons Learned
  • Staff engagement is essential for successful transformation
  • Small, visible improvements build momentum
  • Leadership commitment must be sustained over time
  • Regulatory compliance and PFCC can be reconciled with creativity

Reflection Questions

  1. How do principles of PFCC need to be adapted for long-term care settings compared to acute care?
  2. What strategies can help balance individual preferences with the realities of group living environments?
  3. How might family involvement differ in long-term care compared to other healthcare settings?

Case Study 3: Family Involvement in Adult Mental Health Treatment

Case Overview

The Riverdale Community Mental Health Center implemented a project to enhance family involvement in adult mental health care while respecting patient autonomy and confidentiality. This case study examines their approach to balancing these sometimes competing priorities.

Initial Challenges

  • Concern about privacy and confidentiality under HIPAA regulations
  • Varying patient comfort with family involvement
  • Staff discomfort with family inclusion in treatment planning
  • Lack of structured processes for family engagement
  • Complex family dynamics that sometimes hindered therapeutic processes

PFCC Approaches Implemented

1. Tiered Family Involvement Protocol

Developed a structured approach that allowed patients to specify the level and type of family involvement they preferred, from minimal information sharing to full participation in treatment planning.

2. Family Education Program

Created a series of educational sessions for family members about mental health conditions, treatment approaches, and strategies for supporting recovery.

3. Communication Skills Training

Provided training for staff on effectively engaging family members, facilitating family meetings, and managing complex family dynamics.

4. Family Support Groups

Established peer-led support groups for family members to share experiences, coping strategies, and resources, separate from patient treatment.

Key Innovation: The Family Involvement Spectrum

The center developed a framework that recognized different levels of potential family involvement:

Level Patient Preference Family Role
Level 1 No direct family involvement in treatment Access to general education and support resources only
Level 2 Limited information sharing Receive basic information about diagnosis and treatment plan
Level 3 Moderate involvement Participate in specified treatment components and receive regular updates
Level 4 Full partnership Active participation in all aspects of treatment planning and implementation

Patients could specify different levels for different family members and could change their preferences over time. For patients who lacked capacity to make decisions, appropriate legal protocols were followed.

Outcomes

After 18 months of implementation, the center observed:

  • 70% of patients chose some level of family involvement (Levels 2-4)
  • 25% reduction in emergency visits for patients with Level 3-4 family involvement
  • 30% improvement in medication adherence with family support
  • Increased patient satisfaction with care coordination
  • Lower reported caregiver burden among family members who participated in education and support programs

Challenges and Lessons Learned

Challenges
  • Navigating complex family dynamics and historical conflicts
  • Addressing staff concerns about increased workload
  • Managing expectations when family views differed from patient or treatment team
  • Ensuring consistent implementation across different programs
Lessons Learned
  • Patient-controlled levels of involvement increased comfort with family engagement
  • Structured protocols reduced staff anxiety about privacy violations
  • Family education was valuable even when direct treatment involvement was limited
  • Regular reassessment of involvement preferences was essential

Reflection Questions

  1. How can healthcare providers balance patient autonomy with the benefits of family support in mental health care?
  2. What strategies can help providers navigate situations where family involvement might be both helpful and complicated?
  3. How might the family involvement spectrum approach be adapted to other healthcare contexts?

Case Study 4: PFCC Implementation in a Busy Emergency Department

Case Overview

Memorial Hospital's Emergency Department (ED), serving over 80,000 patients annually, faced challenges with patient satisfaction, staff burnout, and communication breakdowns. This case study examines how they implemented PFCC principles in a high-acuity, fast-paced environment.

Initial Challenges

  • High patient volumes creating pressure for rapid throughput
  • Physical space constraints limiting privacy and family presence
  • Unpredictable workflow and frequent interruptions
  • Staff concerns about family presence during procedures and resuscitations
  • Communication gaps during care transitions and discharge

PFCC Approaches Implemented

1. Family Presence Protocol

Developed guidelines allowing family presence during procedures and resuscitations with dedicated staff support. Included staff education on facilitating family presence and managing challenging situations.

2. Bedside Shift Report

Implemented nurse shift changes at the bedside, including patients and families in the information exchange. Created standardized communication tools to ensure consistency.

3. Care Partner Program

Designated family members as care partners with specific roles and responsibilities during the ED stay. Provided visual identifiers and staff education about engaging care partners.

4. Enhanced Discharge Process

Redesigned discharge instructions using teach-back methodology, involving care partners and ensuring understanding of follow-up care. Implemented post-discharge phone calls for high-risk patients.

Implementation Process

The ED used a phased approach to implementation:

  1. Assessment Phase (2 months): Conducted patient/family focus groups and staff surveys to identify key needs and concerns. Organized an interdisciplinary team including patient/family advisors.
  2. Planning Phase (1 month): Developed implementation plans for each initiative, including necessary resources, education needs, and evaluation methods.
  3. Staff Education (1 month): Provided training on PFCC principles, specific protocols, and communication techniques. Used simulation for family presence during critical events.
  4. Pilot Implementation (3 months): Tested initiatives during day shifts, gathered feedback, and made refinements before full implementation.
  5. Full Implementation (6 months): Rolled out initiatives across all shifts with ongoing coaching and support.

Outcomes

After one year, the ED demonstrated measurable improvements:

Patient/Family Outcomes
  • 22% increase in patient satisfaction scores
  • 35% improvement in discharge instruction comprehension
  • 28% reduction in 72-hour return visits
  • Positive qualitative feedback about family involvement
Staff/Operational Outcomes
  • 15% reduction in staff turnover
  • 8% decrease in time spent responding to family inquiries
  • 10% improvement in medication reconciliation accuracy
  • No increase in length of stay despite additional PFCC elements

Challenges and Lessons Learned

Challenges
  • Initial staff skepticism about feasibility in ED environment
  • Maintaining consistency across varying shifts and patient volumes
  • Adapting for patients without family/care partners present
  • Ensuring privacy in semi-private treatment areas
Lessons Learned
  • PFCC implementation is possible even in fast-paced environments
  • Staff fears about family presence were largely unfounded
  • Clear protocols helped staff navigate complex situations
  • Family involvement often saved time rather than consuming it

Case Study 5: Cultural Considerations in Applying PFCC Principles

Case Overview

Westside Medical Center serves a culturally diverse urban community with significant immigrant populations from East Asia, Middle East, and Central America. This case study examines how the center adapted PFCC approaches to respect cultural diversity while maintaining core principles.

Initial Challenges

  • Diverse cultural perspectives on family roles in healthcare decision-making
  • Language barriers affecting communication and informed consent
  • Varying cultural norms around information disclosure, particularly regarding serious illness
  • Different expectations about gender roles in caregiving and healthcare interactions
  • Limited staff knowledge about specific cultural practices and beliefs

PFCC Approaches Implemented

1. Cultural Assessment Tool

Developed a structured tool for identifying patient and family preferences regarding information sharing, decision-making, and care involvement that was sensitive to cultural factors without making assumptions.

2. Cultural Broker Program

Recruited and trained bilingual/bicultural staff members to serve as cultural brokers who could mediate between healthcare teams and families, providing cultural context and facilitating understanding.

3. Expanded Interpreter Services

Significantly enhanced interpreter availability, including in-person, video, and telephone options available 24/7. Provided staff training on effective work with interpreters.

4. Cultural Competence Training

Implemented ongoing education for all staff focusing on cultural humility, avoiding stereotyping, and recognizing diversity within cultural groups. Included sessions led by community members.

Key Innovation: Culturally-Responsive PFCC Framework

The center developed a framework for adapting PFCC principles across cultures:

PFCC Principle Cultural Considerations Adaptive Approach
Dignity and Respect
  • Variations in greeting practices
  • Different modesty requirements
  • Varied expectations for eye contact
  • Ask about preferences rather than assuming
  • Adapt physical exam approaches
  • Match communication style to expectations
Information Sharing
  • Family-mediated communication
  • Taboos around discussing certain conditions
  • Indirect communication preferences
  • Ask who should receive information
  • Explore communication preferences
  • Use appropriate terminology and metaphors
Participation
  • Varied decision-making hierarchies
  • Different expectations for elder involvement
  • Gender roles in healthcare decisions
  • Identify key decision-makers while including patient
  • Respect family hierarchies
  • Create space for all voices to be heard
Collaboration
  • Different views on healthcare authority
  • Traditional healing practices
  • Varied comfort with questioning providers
  • Acknowledge traditional practices
  • Create safe space for questions
  • Engage community leaders in planning

Case Example: Mr. Nguyen

Mr. Nguyen, a 75-year-old Vietnamese man, was diagnosed with advanced pancreatic cancer. Initial attempts to discuss treatment options directly with him met with uncomfortable silence. The healthcare team applied the culturally-responsive PFCC framework:

  1. Cultural Assessment: Discovered that in his family, serious medical information is typically filtered through the eldest son, who then decides how much to share with the patient.
  2. Adapted Approach: With Mr. Nguyen's permission, the team worked with the eldest son while still including Mr. Nguyen in all meetings. They provided full information to the son and allowed him to guide how information was shared.
  3. Balanced Autonomy and Cultural Practice: The team ensured Mr. Nguyen's consent was obtained and his preferences were honore
  4. Balanced Autonomy and Cultural Practice: The team ensured Mr. Nguyen's consent was obtained and his preferences were honored, while respecting the family's communication structure.
  5. Outcome: The family reported high satisfaction with care, and Mr. Nguyen was able to make informed decisions about palliative options in a way that maintained family harmony.

Outcomes

After two years of implementation, the center observed:

  • 18% increase in patient satisfaction scores among non-English speaking patients
  • 25% reduction in interpreter-related complaints
  • Improved treatment plan adherence across cultural groups
  • Greater diversity in patient and family advisory council membership
  • Higher provider confidence in cross-cultural communication (self-reported)

Challenges and Lessons Learned

Challenges
  • Avoiding stereotyping while acknowledging cultural patterns
  • Balancing respect for cultural practices with patient autonomy
  • Navigating conflicts between cultural practices and organizational policies
  • Limited availability of trained interpreters for certain languages
Lessons Learned
  • Ask rather than assume cultural preferences
  • Recognize diversity within cultural groups
  • Involve community members in program development
  • Balance flexibility with core PFCC principles
